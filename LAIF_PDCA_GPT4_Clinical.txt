LAIF PRE-DEPLOYMENT COHERENCE ASSESSMENT
PDCA-Full  |  Structural Assessment
System Class: GPT-4-Based Clinical Documentation AssistantDeployment Context: Acute Hospital Setting, API Integration
Assessment Scope and Basis  This assessment applies the LAIF v1.2 Pre-Deployment Coherence Assessment (PDCA-Full) to a class of GPT-4 clinical documentation deployments observed between 2023 and 2025, including deployments associated with documented clinical error in the peer-reviewed literature. The findings are based on published system capability assessments, documented failure modes, and the LAIF v1.2 standard as set out in the LAIF Compliance Toolkit v1.1. Findings apply to the deployment configurations described and assessed below. Where GPT-4's properties vary by deployment configuration, that variation is noted. The purpose of the assessment is to demonstrate what the PDCA-Full instrument shows when applied to a consequential AI deployment — including where it reaches a finding of structural incoherence and what redesign it requires.
System Class | GPT-4 Clinical Documentation Assistant (GPT-4 CDA) — large language model system deployed via OpenAI API with prompt engineering and EHR integration for clinical note generation, discharge summaries, referral letters, and diagnostic support outputs.
Deployment Context | Acute hospital inpatient and emergency department settings. Assessed deployment configurations: API integration into EHR workflows, including configurations where AI-generated content is pre-populated into clinical fields without explicit AI provenance labelling.
Operator | Hospital system or clinical software vendor deploying GPT-4 via OpenAI API.
Affected Persons | Patients whose clinical records are generated, summarised, or influenced by the system; clinicians relying on system outputs for treatment decisions.
PDCA Tier | PDCA-Full. Rationale: outputs may materially affect health and treatment decisions of individual patients; deployment in regulated healthcare sector; documented potential for irreversible clinical consequences.

Assessor | Independent structural assessment under LAIF v1.2

SYSTEM DESCRIPTION
The GPT-4 Clinical Documentation Assistant (GPT-4 CDA) refers to the class of deployments in which GPT-4 is integrated via the OpenAI API into hospital electronic health record environments to generate clinical documentation. The system receives structured and unstructured clinical inputs — including physician notes, nursing observations, laboratory results, medication records, and patient-reported information — and generates outputs including clinical progress notes, discharge summaries, referral letters, medication reconciliation summaries, and in some configurations differential diagnosis suggestions.
Between 2023 and 2025, multiple hospital systems and clinical software vendors deployed systems of this type, either through direct API integration or through third-party clinical AI platforms. These deployments occurred in advance of regulatory frameworks adequate to govern them: neither the FDA's Digital Health Center of Excellence guidance nor EU MDR/IVDR requirements had been updated to address the specific properties of general-purpose large language models in clinical documentation contexts.
Published peer-reviewed case reports documented clinical errors attributable to LLM hallucination in clinical note generation across this class of deployments, including: fabrication of laboratory values not present in the source data; omission of documented allergies from discharge summaries; generation of plausible but clinically incorrect medication dosing information; and misattribution of findings from one patient's record to another in multi-patient summary contexts.
Basis for PDCA-Full Classification  This deployment class is assessed under PDCA-Full on four independent grounds: (1) outputs may materially affect the health and treatment decisions of individual patients — the highest-stakes category under LAIF Toolkit Module 7; (2) the deployment is in a regulated sector (healthcare); (3) the documented failure modes satisfy the Provision D1 threshold for very low reversibility — clinical errors producing patient harm cannot be undone; (4) the failure modes documented in the clinical literature are systemic rather than incidental, indicating structural rather than isolated risk.
SECTION A — INTEGRITY LAYER VERIFICATION
This section assesses whether the GPT-4 CDA satisfies the three Integrity Layer properties required as a precondition of lawful deployment under LAIF v1.2, Part Two. All three properties must be satisfied simultaneously. The assessment draws on published capability assessments, documented failure modes, and the operational standards in LAIF Compliance Toolkit v1.1, Module 1. Findings are scoped to the deployment configurations described above.
A.1  Structural Transparency
Operational standard (Toolkit 1.3): The system must be able to produce, on request, a comprehensible account of the basis on which outputs were generated, including the principal factors that influenced the output, the degree of confidence or uncertainty, and the material limitations relevant to the context. The explanation must reliably reflect the actual basis of the output — not a post-hoc rationalisation generated by the same process as the output itself.
✗ | Comprehensible account on request | In the deployment configurations assessed, no mechanism exists for the system to produce a meaningful account of why specific clinical content was generated. Prompt-engineered explanation outputs are post-hoc rationalisations that do not reliably reflect the actual basis of the generative output — a known structural property of transformer-based LLMs not remediated in the assessed configurations by prompt engineering alone.
✗ | Confidence and uncertainty quantification | In standard deployment configurations, the system does not produce calibrated confidence scores for clinical outputs. Clinically incorrect content may be generated with the same linguistic register as correct content. No uncertainty signal is available to the clinician at the point of output in standard EHR integration configurations.
✗ | Material limitations disclosed to operators | Published API documentation and terms of service describe the system's general limitations. However, the specific clinical failure modes documented in peer-reviewed literature — laboratory value hallucination, allergy omission, cross-patient data contamination — are not included in system documentation provided to clinical operators in a form enabling disclosure to patients or treating clinicians at the point of care.
✗ | Explanation reliably reflects actual basis of output | Not satisfied in the assessed configurations. Transformer-based LLMs generate explanations using the same generative process as the original output. An explanation of a hallucinated laboratory value may itself be a confident, fluent, plausible, and incorrect account of why that value was generated — a structural property of the architecture rather than a remediable configuration issue.
A.1 FINDING: STRUCTURAL TRANSPARENCY NOT SATISFIED IN ASSESSED CONFIGURATIONS  In the deployment configurations assessed, the structural transparency requirement of LAIF Integrity Layer 2.1 is not satisfied. The system does not produce meaningful accounts as defined by Toolkit section 1.1. The clinical-specific threshold requires identification of data inputs relied upon, the diagnostic reasoning applied, and the system's known performance rates in comparable populations. None of these are available to clinicians or patients in standard deployment configurations.
A.2  Structural Honesty
Operational standard (Toolkit 1.4): The system's stated optimisation objectives must correspond to its actual implemented objectives. The system must perform consistently whether or not it is being evaluated. The system must not generate outputs misrepresenting its nature, capabilities, or the interests driving its deployment.
✗ | Stated and actual optimisation objectives correspond | The system is optimised for next-token prediction with RLHF fine-tuning for general helpfulness and coherence. In clinical documentation deployments, operators apply additional prompt engineering for format compliance. The system's actual optimisation objective — fluent, contextually plausible text generation — does not correspond to the clinical deployment objective of accurate, complete, and verifiable clinical documentation. This divergence is structural to the architecture and is not addressed by format-compliance prompt engineering.
✗ | Consistent performance in evaluation vs. operational conditions | The system generates plausible clinical text regardless of factual grounding in both evaluation and operational conditions. This property holds consistently — but in the direction of consistent structural limitation: the system cannot distinguish between accurate and inaccurate clinical information in either context. Published studies have shown output quality variation under different framing conditions; however, the more foundational issue for this assessment is the structural inability to prioritise accuracy over fluency.
✗ | System does not misrepresent its nature, capabilities, or limitations | In the assessed deployment configurations, system outputs are integrated into clinical documentation workflows without identification of their AI-generated provenance within the clinical record. The deployment configuration — not the model itself — creates this misrepresentation: the output is presented as clinical documentation rather than as AI-generated text requiring clinical verification.
A.2 FINDING: STRUCTURAL HONESTY NOT SATISFIED IN ASSESSED CONFIGURATIONS  In the deployment configurations assessed, the structural honesty requirement of LAIF Integrity Layer 2.2 is not satisfied. The system's optimisation objective diverges structurally from clinical accuracy. Outputs are presented in clinical documentation contexts without disclosure of their AI-generated nature. The most significant finding is architectural: the system generates clinically plausible text rather than clinically accurate text, and the standard deployment configuration does not communicate this distinction to those who rely on the outputs.
A.3  Structural Containment
Operational standard (Toolkit 1.5): The system must operate within documented operational boundaries in all tested conditions including edge cases. It must not initiate actions with materially irreversible consequences without triggering the appropriate authorisation process. It must surface out-of-scope requests through designated channels rather than attempting to address them unilaterally.
✗ | Operates within documented operational boundaries in all tested conditions | In standard API deployment configurations, the system will attempt to generate a clinical output for any clinical input, including inputs outside any validated operational scope. The system has no mechanism for detecting and declining out-of-scope clinical requests. This is a structural property of the general-purpose model architecture that not reliably resolved through prompt engineering alone in the assessed deployments.
✗ | Surfaces out-of-scope requests through designated channels | Not satisfied in the assessed configurations. The system generates outputs for clinical inputs regardless of whether those inputs fall within validated clinical domains. An out-of-scope specialist request receives a fluent, plausible output rather than a referral flag or scope limitation response.
✗ | Irreversible-consequence actions require appropriate authorisation | Partially satisfied with significant qualification. The system does not directly take clinical actions — it generates text that clinicians then act on. A human review step exists between system output and clinical consequence. However, the documented failure mode of undetected hallucination in high-volume clinical environments means this review step does not consistently interrupt the pathway from output to irreversible clinical consequence. The Provision D1 authorisation process has not been established for this class of deployment.
~ | Agentic system controls (scope limitation, action logging, interrupt mechanisms) | Not applicable in standard deployment configurations. In EHR-integrated configurations where the system automatically populates clinical fields, quasi-agentic properties arise that are not governed by documented scope limitation mechanisms or interrupt processes.
A.3 FINDING: STRUCTURAL CONTAINMENT NOT SATISFIED IN ASSESSED CONFIGURATIONS  In the deployment configurations assessed, the structural containment requirement of LAIF Integrity Layer 2.3 is not satisfied. The system will attempt to generate clinical outputs for any clinical input and has no mechanism for detecting or declining out-of-scope requests. The human review step between system output and clinical action does not reliably interrupt the pathway to irreversible clinical consequence in the high-volume clinical environments where this class of deployment has been documented.
INTEGRITY LAYER FINDING: THRESHOLD NOT SATISFIED IN ASSESSED CONFIGURATIONS  In the deployment configurations assessed, none of the three Integrity Layer properties — structural transparency, structural honesty, or structural containment — are satisfied. Under LAIF v1.2, Part Two, section 2.4, all three properties must be satisfied simultaneously before a system may be deployed in any context governed by the framework. The implications for the Coherence Test are assessed in Section B.
SECTION B — COHERENCE TEST DOCUMENTATION
This section applies the three questions of the Coherence Test to the GPT-4 CDA deployment configurations assessed. A deployment that does not satisfy the Integrity Layer threshold also fails the Coherence Test at Question One, because a deployment that does not protect the basic structural conditions for a governance relationship cannot be said to identify and protect the specific human interest it purports to serve. The three questions are nonetheless applied in full to demonstrate the complete structural assessment.
B.1  Question One — The Coupling Question
Does this deployment identify and protect, with equivalent normative force, the specific human interest it is designed to serve?
Human Interest:  The specific human interest served by this deployment: the patient's interest in receiving clinical documentation that accurately records their clinical status, correctly identifies relevant history (including allergies, contraindications, and prior diagnoses), and supports rather than compromises the clinical decisions of their treating team.
Assessment:  In the assessed deployment configurations, the deployment authorisation — API access and operator prompt engineering — does not generate any corresponding protection of this interest. There is no mechanism ensuring outputs accurately reflect the patient's clinical record. There is no disclosure to patients or treating clinicians that AI is generating documentation. There is no mechanism for patients or clinicians to request a meaningful account of the basis of that documentation. There is no process for contesting AI-generated content subsequently found to be inaccurate.
Coupling Status:  The coupling between the deployment's enabling authorisation and the protection of the specific human interest it purports to serve is absent. The authorisation has normative force; the protection has none — it is not established by the deployment configuration.
B.1 FINDING: COUPLING QUESTION NOT SATISFIED  The deployment does not identify and protect the specific human interest it is designed to serve with equivalent normative force to the deployment authorisation. The specific human interest — accurate, reliable clinical documentation supporting safe clinical decision-making — is not protected by any mechanism in the assessed deployment configurations of equivalent force to the enablement. Under LAIF Principle 2, the deployment cannot proceed as configured.
B.2  Question Two — The Consistency Question
Would the reasoning that justifies this deployment at its current scale also justify comparable deployments at significantly smaller and larger scales?
Governance Logic Under Assessment:  The reasoning permitting this deployment class: that API access constitutes sufficient authorisation for deployment of a general-purpose language model in a clinical documentation context, subject to operator prompt engineering for format compliance.
Consistency at Smaller Scale:  Applied to the smallest comparable actor: a single general practitioner deploying GPT-4 via personal API subscription to generate clinical notes for individual patients, with no validation, no clinical safety review, and no disclosure to patients. The governance logic permits this equally. The result — clinically unsupported AI-generated documentation at the most granular level of clinical practice, with no oversight structure — is incompatible with medical practice regulation and informed consent requirements applied by the same legal systems.
Consistency at Universal Scale:  Applied universally: every clinical documentation task in every healthcare setting is performed by general-purpose language models deployed via API access with no clinical validation, no safety standards, and no disclosure requirement. The aggregate result is systematic replacement of clinically validated documentation with AI-generated text whose accuracy cannot be verified, in the domain where documentation error has the most direct potential for serious and irreversible patient harm.
B.2 FINDING: CONSISTENCY QUESTION NOT SATISFIED  The reasoning justifying this deployment cannot be applied consistently at smaller or larger scales without producing governance outcomes that the same legal and regulatory systems explicitly reject. The deployment class is being treated as exempt from the validation, disclosure, and oversight requirements applied to all other clinical decision-support tools. Under LAIF Principle 5, the deployment cannot proceed on the basis of reasoning that fails this test.
B.3  Question Three — The Reversibility Question
Does this deployment preserve the capacity of future actors to revise or reverse it when evidence, values, or circumstances change?
Reversibility of Individual Consequences:  Clinical errors attributable to LLM hallucination in patient documentation may produce consequences — misdiagnoses incorporated into the medical record, missed allergies leading to adverse reactions, incorrect medication dosing — whose effects on individual patient health are not reversible. The documented failure modes of this deployment class satisfy the LAIF Toolkit section 1.7 definition of substantially irreversible consequences. The applicable precaution threshold under that definition is very high.
Reversibility of Systemic Consequences:  The widespread normalisation of LLM use in clinical documentation across hospital systems has produced a structural governance consequence: the density of existing deployment has made the imposition of ex-ante validation requirements politically and commercially resistant. This constitutes a form of structural lock-in that progressively forecloses the revisability of the governance environment in which future regulatory decisions about clinical AI will be made.
Authorisation Assessment:  The authorisation process required by Provision D1 for actions with substantially irreversible consequences — explicit authorisation at a level proportional to the scale and permanence of foreseeable consequences — was not established in the assessed deployment configurations. Individual hospital deployment decisions did not trigger clinical safety review at a level of institutional authority proportional to the documented patient safety risk.
B.3 FINDING: REVERSIBILITY QUESTION NOT SATISFIED  The deployment produces or risks producing substantially irreversible consequences for individual patients without the authorisation process required by Provision D1. The systemic deployment pattern has also produced structural lock-in engaging Provision D2. Under LAIF Principle 4 and Provision D1, the deployment cannot proceed without authorisation at an appropriate level of clinical governance authority and without compensating protections documented prior to deployment.
SECTION C — PROVISION LAYER COMPLIANCE
C.1  Provision Set A — Human Agency
✗ | Users informed that AI is involved in generating outputs that materially affect their interests | NOT SATISFIED in assessed configurations. Patients are not informed that their clinical documentation is AI-generated. Clinicians in EHR-integrated configurations may not be explicitly informed of the AI provenance of pre-populated fields. Provision A2 Enablement requires every person whose interests are materially affected to be entitled to know an AI system is involved.
✗ | Users can request a meaningful account on demand | NOT SATISFIED in assessed configurations. No mechanism exists for patients or clinicians to request an account of the basis on which AI-generated documentation was produced, the confidence associated with specific clinical content, or the system's known failure modes relevant to the context.
~ | No manipulative, subliminal, or deceptive techniques employed | PARTIALLY SATISFIED. The system does not employ manipulative techniques. However, the presentation of AI-generated documentation as clinical documentation without provenance disclosure constitutes deception by the deployment configuration within the meaning of Provision A2 Constraint — the output creates a false impression of human clinical authorship.
✗ | Users informed of system's material interests and optimisation objectives | NOT SATISFIED in assessed configurations. The system's actual optimisation objective (fluent text generation) is not disclosed to patients or treating clinicians. The commercial interests of the operator in deployment efficiency are not disclosed.
C.2  Provision Set B — Harm Prevention
✓ | System not capable of providing material assistance to mass-casualty weapons development | SATISFIED in this deployment context.
✗ | System tested for discriminatory output patterns in the intended deployment context, with results documented | NOT SATISFIED in assessed configurations. Published studies have documented output quality disparities correlated with patient demographic characteristics in this class of deployment. Operator-level discrimination testing specific to the clinical documentation deployment context and population has not been completed and documented prior to deployment.
~ | Deployment does not concentrate power in ways that foreclose contestation | CONCERN IDENTIFIED. The deployment pattern — a single general-purpose model generating documentation across entire hospital patient populations — concentrates informational power over clinical record creation in ways that have no precedent in clinical documentation practice. Systematic bias, if present, would be difficult for affected patients to identify or contest.
C.3  Provision Set C — Oversight
✓ | System does not take actions designed to obstruct oversight mechanisms | SATISFIED. The system does not actively obstruct oversight.
✗ | Conflicts between operational requirements and Integrity Layer can be surfaced | NOT ESTABLISHED in assessed configurations. No designated channel exists for clinicians who identify hallucinated or inaccurate content to report this as a governance event rather than a product defect.
✗ | No operator configuration deceives oversight authorities, regulatory bodies, or users | NOT SATISFIED in assessed configurations. In configurations where AI-generated documentation is integrated into the EHR without provenance disclosure, the deployment configuration deceives patients, reviewing clinicians, and potentially external parties — including regulatory inspectors — about the nature and authorship of clinical documentation.
✗ | No operator configuration acts against users' interests to advance operator interests | NOT SATISFIED in assessed configurations. Deployment configurations that optimise for documentation speed and throughput metrics at the expense of clinical accuracy satisfy the Toolkit section 1.6 definition of acting against user interests — the system is optimised for metrics the operator knows or ought to know may conflict with patients' objective interests in accurate documentation.
C.4  Provision Set D — Reversibility
✗ | All substantially irreversible actions require authorisation at the appropriate level | NOT SATISFIED in assessed configurations. Deployment authorisation occurred at the level of hospital IT procurement and clinical software vendor agreements — not at the level of clinical governance committees, hospital boards, or regulatory bodies — despite documented potential for substantially irreversible patient harm consequences.
~ | Deployment preserves capacity of future actors to revise it | PARTIALLY SATISFIED. The deployment is technically reversible. However, clinical records already generated and stored in the EHR cannot be retrospectively validated or distinguished from human-authored records in configurations without provenance disclosure.
~ | No governance arrangement permanently forecloses future regulatory revision | CONCERN IDENTIFIED. The normalisation of LLM clinical documentation at scale creates commercial and institutional dependencies that may make mandatory ex-ante validation requirements politically and commercially resistant, engaging the threshold for Provision D2 review.
SECTION D — PDCA SIGN-OFF STATUS
Sign-Off Status  This assessment is a structural assessment under LAIF v1.2 — not a sign-off on the deployment. The following explains why sign-off would not be appropriate for the deployment as assessed, and what would need to be true for sign-off to be possible.
Technical Lead sign-off: Not available in the assessed configuration. Section A identifies three unsatisfied Integrity Layer properties. A Technical Lead completing this PDCA in good faith under LAIF v1.2 cannot confirm Section A technical accuracy, because the deployment configuration does not satisfy the structural transparency, structural honesty, or structural containment requirements.
Legal Counsel sign-off: Not available in the assessed configuration. Section B identifies three unsatisfied Coherence Test questions. A Legal Counsel completing this PDCA cannot confirm Section B legal adequacy, because the coupling, consistency, and reversibility questions all fail.
Senior Responsible Officer authorisation: Not appropriate in the assessed configuration. Under LAIF v1.2, a Senior Responsible Officer authorising a deployment that fails the Integrity Layer and Coherence Test would be in personal structural violation of the Obligation Layer requirements of Part Four, section 4.2.
Independent Reviewer sign-off: Not appropriate in the assessed configuration. The Independent Reviewer would be required to confirm that the coupling documentation identifies a genuine and specific human interest protected with equivalent normative force. That confirmation is not available, because no such protection exists in the current deployment configuration.
ASSESSMENT FINDING
FINDING: DEPLOYMENT AS ASSESSED IS NOT COHERENT UNDER LAIF v1.2  Application of the LAIF v1.2 PDCA-Full to the GPT-4 CDA deployment configurations described produces the following findings. The Integrity Layer threshold is not satisfied in the assessed configurations: none of the three required properties — structural transparency, structural honesty, or structural containment — are met. The Coherence Test fails at all three questions: the deployment does not couple the deployment authorisation to a protection of the patient's interest of equivalent normative force; the governance reasoning does not generalise safely to comparable scales; and the deployment produces or risks producing substantially irreversible consequences without appropriate authorisation. The Provision Layer shows material failures across Provision Sets A, B, C, and D. The conclusion is structural: the deployment as configured is not coherent under LAIF v1.2. This does not mean AI-assisted clinical documentation is structurally incompatible with LAIF — it means this deployment configuration is. Section below describes what a coherent configuration would require.
REDESIGN REQUIREMENTS
The following requirements must be satisfied before the deployment can be resubmitted for PDCA assessment. They are derived directly from the findings above and are stated in order of structural priority.
1.  Pre-Deployment Clinical Safety Validation  The system must undergo validation against clinical safety standards appropriate to its intended deployment domain before integration into clinical documentation workflows. Validation must include testing against representative patient populations including demographic subgroups; documentation of false positive and false negative rates for specific clinical content types (laboratory values, medication dosing, allergy documentation); and independent clinical review of validation methodology and results. Validation must be completed prior to deployment, not concurrently with it.
2.  Mandatory AI Provenance Disclosure  All clinical documentation generated or substantially influenced by the system must be identified as AI-generated within the clinical record at the point of generation and in all subsequent views of that documentation. This disclosure must be visible to treating clinicians, to patients who request access to their records, and to any external parties with legitimate access to the clinical record. The disclosure mechanism must be tamper-resistant and must persist through any subsequent editing of AI-generated content.
3.  Meaningful Account Mechanism  A mechanism must be established through which clinicians can, on request, obtain a specification of: the data inputs the system relied upon in generating a specific output; the system's known performance metrics for the content type in question; and the recommendation to subject the output to clinical verification before it is treated as authoritative. This mechanism must be accessible within the clinical workflow.
4.  Structured Contestation Pathway  A documented process must be established through which clinicians who identify errors in AI-generated documentation can flag the specific error, request amendment of the clinical record, and report the error to the operator as a governance event. Error reports must be aggregated and analysed for systematic failure patterns, with findings used to update the PDCA and trigger Material Change Review under Toolkit Module 3.
5.  Clinical Governance Authorisation  The deployment must be authorised by the hospital's clinical governance committee or equivalent body — at a level of institutional authority proportional to the patient safety risk — rather than at the level of IT procurement and software vendor agreements. The authorisation must document: the specific clinical domains in which the system is validated; the patient populations for which validation has been completed; the risk mitigation measures in place; and the conditions under which the deployment will be suspended.
6.  Patient Disclosure Protocol  Patients must be informed, before their clinical documentation is generated by AI systems, that AI is involved in creating their clinical records and what their rights are in relation to that documentation. This disclosure must be provided in accessible language and must include the right to request human clinical review of AI-generated documentation.
7.  Discrimination Testing in Deployment Context  The system must be tested for discriminatory output patterns specific to the clinical documentation context before deployment, assessing quality, completeness, and accuracy across patient demographic groups defined by the protected characteristics in LAIF Provision B2. Results must be documented and reviewed by the clinical governance committee as part of the authorisation process.
CONDITIONS FOR A COHERENT DEPLOYMENT
The assessment above does not conclude that AI-assisted clinical documentation is structurally incompatible with LAIF. It concludes that the specific deployment configurations assessed are not coherent under LAIF v1.2. The following describes the minimum conditions under which a deployment of this class could satisfy the framework.
Integrity Layer — Satisfied Configuration  Structural transparency can be satisfied by: a companion transparency layer that logs, for each AI-generated output, the specific source data fields accessed and their content at time of generation; a disclosure of the system's validated performance metrics (accuracy rates by clinical content type and patient demographic); and a patient-accessible summary of these properties. Structural honesty can be satisfied by: system configuration that identifies outputs as AI-generated at the point of generation; documentation of the system's actual optimisation objective (text fluency) as distinct from its clinical application objective (documentation accuracy); and implementation of a confidence flag that surfaces content types with elevated known error rates. Structural containment can be satisfied by: scope limitation to validated clinical domains with explicit refusal of out-of-scope requests; logging of all generated outputs sufficient to support post-hoc clinical audit; and interrupt mechanisms allowing clinical governance teams to suspend the system across all deployment instances.
Coherence Test — Satisfied Configuration  Coupling is satisfied when the deployment authorisation is paired with formal protections of the patient's interest in accurate clinical documentation of equivalent normative force — specifically, the mandatory AI provenance disclosure, the meaningful account mechanism, and the structured contestation pathway described in the Redesign Requirements above. Consistency is satisfied when the validation, disclosure, and oversight requirements applied to this deployment are the same requirements that would be applied to any other clinical decision-support tool seeking to influence clinical documentation in the same hospital setting. Reversibility is satisfied when the clinical governance authorisation documents the conditions under which the deployment will be suspended and when the system is not integrated in configurations that prevent subsequent validation of AI-generated records.
Provision Layer — Key Conditions  Provision A1: Patients informed at admission or first contact that AI is involved in generating their clinical documentation; mechanism available to request human-only documentation on request. Provision A2: Clinician and patient disclosure of AI provenance and optimisation objective; no deployment in configurations where AI provenance is not visible in the clinical record. Provision B2: Discrimination testing completed prior to deployment; results reviewed by clinical governance; automated monitoring for demographic performance disparities in ongoing deployment. Provision C3: No deployment configuration that optimises for speed or throughput metrics in ways the operator knows may conflict with documentation accuracy. Provision D1: Clinical governance committee authorisation obtained and documented; authorisation specifies risk mitigation measures and suspension conditions.
