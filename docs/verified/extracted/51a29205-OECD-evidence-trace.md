## Section A — Integrity Layer

### A.1 Structural Transparency — STRONG

**Primary evidence sections:**
- Principle 1.3 — Transparency and explainability (the core provision requiring AI actors to provide meaningful information about AI systems and their outputs)
- Section on Principle 2 — Policies and international co-operation (particularly on transparency in governance)

**Finding basis:** The Recommendation explicitly requires AI actors to commit to transparency, including meaningful information accessible to affected persons. This aligns structurally with LAIF §2.1 Structural Transparency.

**Limitation noted in assessment:** The Recommendation does not operationalise a triggerable on-request account mechanism as specified in LAIF §2.1.

- Principle 2.1 — Investing in responsible AI R&D

**Finding basis:** The Recommendation addresses performance consistency and safety requirements, which partially map to LAIF §2.2 Structural Honesty. The correspondence between stated and implemented objectives is not directly addressed.

### A.3 Structural Containment — STRONG

**Primary evidence sections:**
- Principle 1.5 — Accountability (including mechanisms for AI actors to be held responsible)
- Principle 2.2 — Fostering a digital ecosystem for AI (including human oversight requirements)

**Finding basis:** The Recommendation's accountability principle and human oversight provisions address the core LAIF §2.3 containment requirement — that AI systems must operate within documented boundaries and support human control.

## Section B — Coherence Test

### Q1 Coupling — Q1a EXPLICIT / Q1b SOFT

**Primary evidence sections:**
- Principle 1.1 — Inclusive growth, sustainable development, and well-being (naming the human interest protected)
- Principle 1.2 — Human-centred values and fairness (naming the specific human interests at risk from AI)
- Principle 1.3 — Transparency and explainability (pairing transparency restriction with information-right protection)
- Principle 1.4 — Robustness, security and safety (pairing safety obligation with harm protection)
- Principle 1.5 — Accountability (pairing accountability obligation with affected-person interest)

**Q1a (EXPLICIT) basis:** Each of the five core principles explicitly names both the obligation and the specific human interest it protects. The coupling architecture is structurally visible.

**Q1b (SOFT) basis:** The Recommendation is non-binding. There is no enforcement mechanism accessible to affected persons equivalent to the obligations imposed on AI actors. The force derives from political commitment and reputational accountability among member states.

**LAIF §2.4 reference:** Equivalent normative force requires enforceable rights, not merely aspirational norms. The Recommendation fails this threshold, producing Q1b = SOFT.

---

### Q2 Consistency — PASS

**Primary evidence sections:**
- Section on scope of adherence (46 adherents)
- Principles 2.1–2.5 (the policy-commitment provisions applied to governments as actors)

**Finding basis:** The Recommendation applies the same governance logic to all actors — private operators, government actors, and international bodies — without carve-outs. The logic survives scale-invariance testing: applied to both individual AI deployments and national-level AI strategies, the same coupling architecture holds.

---

### Q3 Reversibility — PASS

**Primary evidence sections:**
- Section on review mechanism (mandated periodic review)
- Structure of recommendation as recommendation (non-binding; national implementation discretionary)

**Finding basis:** The Recommendation explicitly provides for mandated review. As a non-binding instrument, it preserves future actors' capacity to revise implementation. No provision creates structural irreversibility.
