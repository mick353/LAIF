# LAIF Calibration and Score Justification

## Purpose

This document defines the interpretive boundary for LAIF score calibration and score justification metadata. It explains how deterministic rubric scores should be read in reports without changing scoring logic, validation behavior, formal LAIF-native certification, governance controls, protected artifacts, or verified-source materials.

## What LAIF Scores Are

LAIF scores are deterministic rubric outputs. They identify LAIF-model signal strength across structural governance architecture, canonical terminology, conceptual proximity, auditability, enforceability, sector context, source evidence traces, and remediation metadata.

Scores support diagnostic triage, comparative review, remediation prioritization, and reviewer explanation. They are useful only when interpreted with the assessment mode, fired and missed signals, source evidence/fallback status, sector profile context, remediation patches, known calibration limits, and anti-gaming caveats.

## What LAIF Scores Are Not

LAIF scores are not legal determinations. They are not regulatory compliance ratings, not safety certification, not proof of governance validity, and not proof of enforceability.

Scores do not certify LAIF-native compliance. Formal LAIF-native certification remains governed by the strict binary certification boundary. Scores do not determine external legal validity, governance validity, safety, or enforceability under another authority.

High conceptual, sector, or evidence proximity cannot override formal LAIF-native failure. FORMAL FAIL + HIGH SEMANTIC / SECTOR / EVIDENCE PROXIMITY = FORMAL FAIL.

## Assessment Mode Boundary

Assessment mode controls interpretation. In LAIF-native certification mode, canonical LAIF structures and terms remain load-bearing for the certification gate. In external framework assessment mode, scores are diagnostic mapping metadata only and do not certify the source as LAIF-native.

A low score may indicate missing LAIF-model signals, not necessarily legal invalidity under the source framework's own authority. A high score may reflect text signals and still require evidence, authority, responsible actor, trigger, control, reversibility, consequence, and auditability verification.

## Score Components

The score components are structural governance architecture, canonical terminology, conceptual proximity, auditability, enforceability, overall readiness, sector risk alignment, evidence trace metadata, and remediation patch metadata.

Score bands should be read as LAIF-model signal strength: minimal, limited, partial, substantial, or strong LAIF-model signal. They should not be restated as compliance bands or legal verdicts.

## Fired and Missed Signal Interpretation

Fired signals explain which configured rubric labels were detected. Missed signals explain which configured rubric labels were not detected. They support score justification, but they do not prove real-world implementation.

Rubric labels must be used as reviewer-facing explanations, not as raw pattern disclosures or keyword-stuffing instructions. Reviewers should ask whether the source has structural evidence: actor, trigger, control, evidence, reversibility, escalation, consequence, and auditability.

## Calibration Limits

The current rubric has deterministic limits. It may under-recognize equivalent legal or policy language that does not match configured signals. It may over-recognize dense textual signals that are not supported by implementation evidence.

Scores are not statistical confidence values. No empirical or statistical validation claim is made for score weights, thresholds, or readiness bands.

## False Positive / False Negative Risks

A false positive risk exists when text contains high signal density but lacks structural implementation evidence, accountable authority, control ownership, reviewability, consequence, or audit records.

A false negative risk exists when a source uses different legal, regulatory, or institutional language that may be meaningful under its own authority but does not activate LAIF-model signals.

Neither risk should be turned into a legal claim. Both require reviewer source analysis and authority-specific review.

## Anti-Gaming Boundary

Rubric visibility must not be treated as permission for keyword stuffing. Keyword stuffing without structural evidence is a gaming risk.

Anti-gaming review should focus on structural evidence, not signal density alone. Dense terminology, conceptual overlap, sector language, or evidence snippets must be checked against actor, trigger, control, evidence, reversibility, escalation, consequence, and auditability. A gaming-risk note is not a finding of bad faith and not a legal invalidity claim.

## Evidence Trace Relationship

Evidence traces support exact source presence where available and identify fallback-required source-support gaps where extraction cannot provide an exact quote. Evidence traces do not prove implementation.

Trace presence cannot override formal LAIF-native failure, and fallback-only traces require reviewer confirmation before operational reliance.

## Sector Profile Relationship

Sector profiles contextualize diagnostics by surfacing sector-relevant interests, risk indicators, evidence expectations, and remediation themes. They do not create sector compliance gates.

High sector alignment means the source contains sector-relevant signals. It does not prove sector compliance, safety, legal validity, or implementation.

## Remediation Patch Relationship

Remediation patches are diagnostic unless separately adopted by an authority. They do not determine legal validity, certify LAIF-native compliance, or create mandatory legal amendments on their own.

Patch records should be read as structured reviewer guidance linking diagnostic gaps to possible controls, evidence artifacts, verification tests, responsible actors, and authority boundaries.

## Reporting Boundary

Reports may include score interpretation, score justification, dimension justifications, calibration cautions, and gaming risk notes. These are interpretive metadata only.

Reports must not expose raw regex patterns, publish a keyword-stuffing recipe, or imply that high scores certify LAIF-native compliance. Reports should state: Score justification explains LAIF-model signal strength only. It does not determine legal validity or certify LAIF-native compliance.

## Future Calibration Work

Future calibration work may add reviewer fixtures, source-language examples, empirical checks, sector-specific evidence studies, or anti-gaming stress tests. Such work should remain separate from formal LAIF-native certification boundaries unless explicitly adopted through governance review.

Future work should preserve the formal invariant: high semantic, sector, or evidence proximity cannot convert formal LAIF-native failure into a pass.
