# LAIF Governance-Force Model

LAIF's practical institutional-assessment lens is that **LAIF assesses whether governance principles are operationalised into governance force**. A document can endorse admirable principles and still leave institutional actors without enforceable duties, evidence artifacts, reversal paths, or accountability mechanisms.

Governance force asks whether a reader can identify who must do what, when, for whose protection, with what proof, under what escalation path, and with what consequence if the obligation is ignored.

## Governance-Force Components

| Component | Diagnostic question |
|---|---|
| mandate | Is the obligation stated as `must`, `shall`, `required`, or equivalent mandatory language, or is it only aspirational? |
| actor | Who is responsible for performing, approving, reviewing, escalating, or enforcing the obligation? |
| trigger | When does the obligation activate: design, procurement, deployment, substantial modification, incident, high-risk use, complaint, audit, or renewal? |
| protected interest | What human or public interest is protected, such as health, safety, liberty, equality, privacy, due process, access to services, employment opportunity, or public accountability? |
| control | What must be done: assessment, documentation, testing, monitoring, approval, human review, suspension, remediation, disclosure, or procurement gate? |
| evidence | What artifact proves it was done: impact assessment, audit log, test report, approval record, model card, incident register, risk acceptance, vendor attestation, or remediation record? |
| reversibility | How can decisions, consequences, deployments, model behavior, or institutional effects be corrected, appealed, modified, suspended, withdrawn, or reversed? |
| escalation | What happens under uncertainty, breach, exception, incident, contestation, or high risk? |
| consequence | What happens if the obligation is ignored: denial of approval, procurement exclusion, suspension, remediation order, reporting duty, accountability review, or other sanction? |
| auditability | Can an independent reviewer verify whether the obligation existed, activated, was performed, produced evidence, and led to consequences when ignored? |

## Mapping Governance Force to LAIF Constructs

| LAIF construct | Governance-force role |
|---|---|
| Coupling | Links a restriction, deployment, or decision to the protected human/public interest and requires protection of equivalent normative force. |
| Integrity Layer | Establishes threshold conditions for structural transparency, structural honesty, and structural containment before a system or governance instrument can be treated as reliable. |
| Coherence Test | Tests whether the governance logic protects interests, generalises consistently, and preserves reversibility. |
| Structural Transparency | Requires explanations, records, limitations, and reviewable accounts that make audit possible. |
| Structural Honesty | Requires stated objectives, incentives, and implemented behavior to align so governance is not performative or misleading. |
| Structural Containment | Requires boundaries, authorization gates, and limits that prevent uncontrolled or irreversible action. |
| Consistency | Requires comparable actors and scales to be governed by workable, non-arbitrary logic. |
| Reversibility | Requires institutional capacity to correct, modify, appeal, suspend, withdraw, or remediate decisions and consequences. |

## Institutional Examples

### Government AI policy

A government AI policy has governance force when it identifies mandatory duties for public bodies, names accountable officials, defines triggers for high-risk AI use, protects public interests such as due process and equality, requires impact assessments and monitoring records, preserves appeal or correction routes, escalates incidents, and blocks or remediates non-compliant deployments.

### Departmental AI development lifecycle policy

A departmental lifecycle policy has governance force when design, data selection, model evaluation, deployment approval, monitoring, change management, incident response, and retirement each have required controls, responsible actors, evidence artifacts, review gates, and reversal mechanisms.

### Procurement/vendor governance

Procurement and vendor governance have force when tenders, contracts, and renewals require vendor disclosures, testing evidence, audit rights, incident reporting, data-use limits, human oversight, remediation duties, suspension rights, and consequences such as exclusion, cure periods, withholding approval, or contract termination.

### Clinical AI policy

Clinical AI policy has governance force when patient safety, informed use, clinical accountability, adverse-event escalation, validation evidence, monitoring, override rights, patient redress, model update controls, and withdrawal procedures are mandatory and independently auditable.

### Employment/HR AI policy

Employment and HR AI policy has governance force when it protects applicants and workers from unfair exclusion, discrimination, opacity, and unreviewable automation. It should define accountable HR or governance actors, triggers for hiring or performance tools, audit evidence, human review, contestation rights, escalation for bias or error, and consequences for unauthorized or harmful use.

## Interpretation Boundary

Governance-force analysis is a LAIF model lens. It can identify structural gaps, ambiguity, or remediation priorities, but it does not determine legal validity, regulator acceptance, industry compliance, or the legal enforceability of an external instrument under its own authority.
