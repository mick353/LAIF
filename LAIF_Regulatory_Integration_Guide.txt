LAIF REGULATORY INTEGRATION GUIDE
Step-by-Step Plug-In for EU AI Act and US Federal Requirements  |  April 2026
A practical guide for AI providers, operators, and regulators integrating LAIF v1.2into existing compliance workflows — without displacing existing frameworks
Purpose  This guide answers a specific practical question: how does an organisation that is already subject to existing AI regulation — the EU AI Act, US federal AI requirements, or sector-specific frameworks — integrate LAIF v1.2 into its existing compliance processes? The answer is: without displacing anything. LAIF sits at constitutional level; existing frameworks sit at statutory level. The PDCA is designed to satisfy and extend existing documentation requirements, not replace them. This guide shows exactly where and how the integration points work, what LAIF adds that existing frameworks do not provide, and the step-by-step process for each regulatory context.
PART ONE — EU AI ACT INTEGRATION
This part describes how a provider of a high-risk AI system under Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 integrates LAIF v1.2 into their existing EU AI Act compliance process. It assumes the provider has identified their system as high-risk under Annex III and is working through the conformity assessment process. LAIF integration adds approximately three additional documentation steps and one additional governance mechanism to the existing conformity assessment workflow.
1.1  The Existing Conformity Assessment Workflow
A provider working through EU AI Act conformity assessment for an Annex III high-risk AI system is already required to complete the following obligations, which LAIF integrates with rather than replaces:
Article | Obligation | Scope
Article 9 | Risk Management System | Identify and analyse known and reasonably foreseeable risks throughout the lifecycle. Document the risk management system. Review and update it.
Article 10 | Data and Data Governance | Ensure training, validation, and testing datasets are subject to appropriate data governance practices. Examine data for biases. Ensure datasets are relevant, representative, and sufficiently free of errors.
Article 13 | Transparency to Deployers | Provide deployers with information enabling them to understand the system's capabilities, limitations, and intended purpose. Provide instructions for use.
Article 14 | Human Oversight | Design and develop the system to enable effective human oversight. Implement appropriate oversight measures.
Article 15 | Accuracy, Robustness, Cybersecurity | Achieve appropriate levels of accuracy, robustness, and cybersecurity. Document performance metrics.
Article 17 | Quality Management System | Establish, document, and implement a quality management system covering all obligations under Chapter III Section 2.
Article 43 | Conformity Assessment | Either self-assess (Annex VI) or undergo third-party conformity assessment (Annex VII, for certain biometric systems). Register in the EU database.
Article 86 | Right to Explanation | Provide affected persons with an explanation of the role of the AI system in the decision-making process and the main elements of the decision taken.
1.2  Where LAIF Integrates — Provision-by-Provision Mapping
The table below maps each LAIF PDCA section to the EU AI Act obligation it satisfies or extends, identifies what LAIF adds that the EU AI Act does not provide, and identifies where the EU AI Act's existing standard is weaker than the LAIF standard.
LAIF PDCA Section | EU AI Act Article(s) | LAIF Satisfies/Extends | What LAIF Adds (Gap)
PDCA Section A.1Structural Transparency | Arts 13, 86 | SATISFIES and EXTENDS. Article 13 requires transparency to deployers; Article 86 provides a right to explanation in certain contexts. LAIF Section A.1 verification confirms the system can produce a meaningful account for any materially affected person. | LAIF's 'meaningful account' standard is non-waivable and applies to all persons materially affected — not only in the specific contexts defined by Article 86. LAIF also requires that explanations reliably reflect the actual basis of the output, which Article 86 does not explicitly require.
PDCA Section A.2Structural Honesty | Art 13, Art 17 | SATISFIES and EXTENDS. Article 13 requires accurate capability representation to deployers. Article 17 requires the quality management system to cover all documentation accurately. | LAIF explicitly requires that the system's actual optimisation objective corresponds to its stated objective — a requirement that goes beyond Article 13's deployer-facing disclosure. LAIF also requires consistency under adversarial conditions, which no EU AI Act article specifically addresses.
PDCA Section A.3Structural Containment | Art 14, Art 15 | SATISFIES and EXTENDS. Article 14 requires human oversight capabilities; Article 15 requires robustness. LAIF Section A.3 verification confirms the system operates within documented operational boundaries in all tested conditions including edge cases and adversarial inputs. | LAIF's scope limitation requirement — that the system declines out-of-scope requests rather than attempting to address them — is more specific than Article 14's human oversight requirement. LAIF also requires interrupt mechanisms allowing human principals to halt the system, which Article 14 addresses in principle but does not operationally specify.
PDCA Section B.1Coupling Documentation | Art 9 (risk identification) | EXTENDS. Article 9 requires identification of known and reasonably foreseeable risks. LAIF Section B.1 requires identification of the specific human interest protected by the deployment and confirmation that the protection has equivalent normative force to the deployment authorisation. | The EU AI Act does not require that risks be coupled to corresponding protections of equivalent normative force. It identifies risks; LAIF requires that for every restriction, the specific interest that restriction serves must be identified and protected. This is the most significant structural gap: the EU AI Act is asymmetric (strong prohibitions, weaker protection mechanisms); LAIF is symmetric.
PDCA Section B.2Consistency Documentation | Art 9 (risk assessment), Art 17 | EXTENDS. Article 9 requires risk assessment; Article 17 requires consistent quality management. LAIF Section B.2 requires that the governance logic applies at smaller and larger scales. | The EU AI Act does not require consistency testing across actor scales. A large platform and a small developer are subject to the same risk-tier classification but potentially very different practical compliance burdens. LAIF's consistency question specifically addresses this — the governing logic must be defensible for all actors subject to the same authorisation.
PDCA Section B.3Reversibility Documentation | Art 9 (residual risk) | EXTENDS. Article 9 requires assessment of residual risk. LAIF Section B.3 requires identification of all substantially irreversible consequences and confirmation that authorisation at the appropriate level has been obtained. | The EU AI Act treats irreversibility as a risk factor; LAIF treats it as a governance threshold that triggers a mandatory authorisation process. The EU AI Act has no equivalent of LAIF's authorisation-level calibration to the permanence of foreseeable consequences.
PDCA Section C.1Provision Set A (Human Agency) | Arts 13, 14, 86 | SATISFIES and EXTENDS. Article 86 provides a right to explanation; Articles 13 and 14 address transparency and human oversight. | LAIF's Provision A1 entitlement is non-waivable and cannot be made conditional on fees — Article 86 does not include this protection. LAIF's Provision A2 requires disclosure that an AI system is involved regardless of how the system is presented — Article 86 applies only in defined contexts.
PDCA Section C.2Provision Set B (Harm Prevention) | Art 5 (prohibitions), Art 10 (data bias) | SATISFIES. Article 5's categorical prohibitions are consistent with LAIF Provision B1. Article 10's data governance requirements are consistent with LAIF Provision B2. | LAIF extends Article 10 by requiring discrimination testing in the specific deployment context rather than only in training data. LAIF Provision B3 (prohibition on power concentration) has no direct equivalent in the EU AI Act.
PDCA Section C.3Provision Set C (Oversight) | Arts 14, 17 | SATISFIES and EXTENDS. Article 14 requires human oversight; Article 17 requires quality management. | LAIF Provision C3 — the prohibition on operator configuration that acts against user interests to advance operator interests — has no direct equivalent in the EU AI Act. This is identified in the LAIF Case Analysis as the most practically important provision: harms often arise from deployment incentives, not model capability.
PDCA Section DSign-Off Requirements | Art 17 (QMS documentation) | SATISFIES and EXTENDS. Article 17 requires documentation of the quality management system including responsibilities and authorities. | LAIF's sign-off structure — requiring Technical Lead, Legal Counsel, Senior Responsible Officer, and Independent Reviewer — is more specific and cross-functional than Article 17's documentation requirement. The Independent Reviewer requirement for PDCA-Full has no equivalent in EU AI Act conformity assessment for most Annex III systems.
1.3  Step-by-Step Integration Process for EU AI Act Providers
The following process describes how a provider completing EU AI Act conformity assessment for an Annex III high-risk AI system integrates LAIF into their existing workflow. Steps marked [EXISTING] are already required by the EU AI Act. Steps marked [LAIF] are additional steps that LAIF adds. Steps marked [LAIF EXTENDS] are existing steps that LAIF deepens.
Step | LAIF Action | Existing EU Obligation | Deliverable | EU Output | Timeline
Step 1 | [EXISTING] Identify System as High-RiskDetermine whether the system falls within Annex III using existing EU AI Act classification guidance. | EU Art 6 + Annex III classification | System classification documented | EU Art 6 + Annex III classification | Before development begins
Step 2 | [LAIF] Determine PDCA TierApply the PDCA tiering criteria from LAIF Toolkit Module 7 to determine whether PDCA-Full, PDCA-Standard, or PDCA-Lite applies. For Annex III high-risk systems, PDCA-Full is required in all cases. | LAIF Toolkit Module 7 | PDCA tier documented | LAIF Toolkit Module 7 | Before development begins
Step 3 | [LAIF EXTENDS] Article 9 Risk Management → PDCA Section A + B.3Complete the Article 9 risk management process AND complete LAIF PDCA Sections A (Integrity Layer Verification) and B.3 (Reversibility Documentation). The PDCA Section A verification satisfies and extends Article 9. The reversibility documentation satisfies and extends Article 9's residual risk assessment. | Art 9 + LAIF PDCA Sections A and B.3 | Risk management system documented with LAIF verification evidence | Art 9 + LAIF PDCA Sections A and B.3 | During development phase
Step 4 | [LAIF EXTENDS] Article 10 Data Governance → PDCA Section C.2Complete the Article 10 data governance process AND complete LAIF PDCA Section C.2 (Provision Set B — Harm Prevention). The PDCA Section C.2 discrimination testing requirement satisfies and extends Article 10's bias examination requirement, and additionally requires testing in the specific deployment context rather than only in training data. | Art 10 + LAIF PDCA Section C.2 | Data governance documentation with deployment-context discrimination testing results | Art 10 + LAIF PDCA Section C.2 | During development phase
Step 5 | [LAIF] Coupling Documentation — PDCA Section B.1Complete LAIF PDCA Section B.1 (Coupling Documentation). This has no direct EU AI Act equivalent. The provider must identify the specific human interest the system is designed to serve and confirm that a protection of equivalent normative force exists. This step frequently surfaces structural gaps that are not identified by the EU AI Act risk management process alone. | LAIF PDCA Section B.1 | Coupling documentation: human interest identified; equivalent protection confirmed | LAIF PDCA Section B.1 | During development phase
Step 6 | [LAIF] Consistency Documentation — PDCA Section B.2Complete LAIF PDCA Section B.2 (Consistency Documentation). This has no direct EU AI Act equivalent. The provider must confirm that the governance logic applies defensibly at smaller and larger scales. For providers whose systems will be used by a range of deployers of different sizes, this step often reveals that the governance logic is calibrated to large, well-resourced deployers and is not defensible for smaller deployers. | LAIF PDCA Section B.2 | Consistency documentation: scale analysis completed | LAIF PDCA Section B.2 | During development phase
Step 7 | [LAIF EXTENDS] Article 13 Transparency → PDCA Section C.1 + A.1Complete the Article 13 transparency documentation AND confirm LAIF PDCA Section A.1 (Structural Transparency) and Section C.1 (Provision Set A — Human Agency). The LAIF standard is more demanding: it requires a meaningful account mechanism satisfying all four criteria in Toolkit section 1.1, which extends beyond Article 13's deployer-facing transparency requirement to a non-waivable patient/user-facing disclosure obligation. | Art 13 + LAIF PDCA Sections A.1 and C.1 | Transparency documentation; meaningful account mechanism described | Art 13 + LAIF PDCA Sections A.1 and C.1 | Before deployment
Step 8 | [LAIF EXTENDS] Article 17 Quality Management System → PDCA Section DComplete the Article 17 quality management system documentation AND complete LAIF PDCA Section D (Sign-Off Requirements). The PDCA sign-off structure — Technical Lead, Legal Counsel, Senior Responsible Officer, Independent Reviewer — satisfies and extends Article 17's documentation requirement for responsibilities and authorities. | Art 17 + LAIF PDCA Section D | QMS documentation with PDCA sign-off records | Art 17 + LAIF PDCA Section D | Before deployment
Step 9 | [EXISTING] Article 43 Conformity AssessmentComplete the applicable conformity assessment (self-assessment under Annex VI or third-party assessment under Annex VII). Submit the PDCA as part of the technical documentation. The PDCA satisfies the documentation requirements for Articles 9, 10, 13, 14, 15, and 17. | Art 43 + Annexes VI/VII | Conformity assessment completed; EU database registration | Art 43 + Annexes VI/VII | Before market placement

The LAIF protocol is more specific: it requires discrimination monitoring (minimum quarterly), containment integrity review (minimum monthly), and annual PDCA review. These satisfy and extend the Article 9 requirement. | Art 9 (post-market) + LAIF Toolkit Module 3 | Monitoring regime operational; annual PDCA review scheduled | Art 9 (post-market) + LAIF Toolkit Module 3 | After deployment, continuously
What LAIF Adds to EU AI Act Compliance — In Plain Terms  The EU AI Act tells providers what to document and verify. LAIF tells providers why each documentation requirement exists — specifically, what human interest each requirement is designed to protect — and requires them to confirm that the protection is as strong as the restriction. This sounds simple, but it is the most common gap in current EU AI Act compliance practice: providers document their risks but do not document with equivalent rigour what protects the persons who bear those risks. LAIF's Section B.1 coupling documentation is the single step most likely to surface structural gaps that existing EU AI Act compliance processes miss.
PART TWO — US FEDERAL REQUIREMENTS INTEGRATION
This part describes how an AI provider or federal agency integrates LAIF v1.2 into US federal AI governance requirements. The US federal framework is less prescriptive and more fragmented than the EU AI Act — it comprises executive orders, OMB memoranda, and sector-specific regulatory guidance rather than a single comprehensive statute. LAIF integrates with this landscape differently: it provides a unifying structural standard that can be applied across the disparate federal requirements without requiring new enabling legislation.
2.1  The US Federal AI Governance Landscape
The relevant US federal AI governance requirements, as of April 2026, are as follows. LAIF integrates with each.
Instrument | Title | Scope
OMB M-24-10 | Advancing Governance, Innovation, and Risk Management for Agency Use of AI | Requires federal agencies to designate a Chief AI Officer; conduct annual inventories of AI use cases; apply responsible AI practices; conduct impact assessments for AI systems affecting rights or safety; and maintain minimum practices for safety-impacting AI.
OMB M-24-18 | Advancing the Responsible Acquisition of Artificial Intelligence in Government | Establishes requirements for federal procurement of AI systems including vendor accountability, data transparency, risk management documentation, and minimum contractual terms for AI procurements.
NIST AI RMF 1.0 | AI Risk Management Framework | Voluntary framework providing guidance for AI risk identification, assessment, and mitigation across the Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage functions. Not legally binding, but cited in federal agency AI governance policies as the preferred operational framework.
EO 14110 / Successor Orders | Executive Orders on Safe, Secure, and Trustworthy AI | Established reporting requirements for dual-use foundation model developers, directed sector-specific regulatory bodies to develop AI safety guidance, and established the AI Safety Institute at NIST.
FISMA / FedRAMP | Federal Information Security Modernization Act / Federal Risk and Authorization Management Program | Establishes cybersecurity and risk management requirements for federal information systems including AI systems deployed in federal environments. Relevant to Structural Containment (PDCA Section A.3).
2.2  LAIF–NIST AI RMF Integration: Function-by-Function
The NIST AI RMF is the primary operational framework for US federal AI risk management. The table below maps each NIST AI RMF function to the LAIF PDCA sections that satisfy it, and identifies what LAIF adds.
NIST AI RMF Function | What It Requires | LAIF PDCA Satisfies | What LAIF Adds
GOVERN(Policies and Accountability) | Establish and communicate policies, processes, roles, and responsibilities for AI risk management. Ensure organisational accountability for AI risk. Integrate AI risk management into enterprise risk management. | PDCA Section D (Sign-Off Requirements) — establishes documented accountability at technical, legal, and senior leadership levels. LAIF Obligation Layer (Part Four) — defines duties of operators and regulators. LAIF Module 7 corporate governance integration provides the board-level mechanism. | LAIF's Obligation Layer makes explicit that operator duties include not configuring AI systems against user interests — a governance accountability requirement that NIST AI RMF's Govern function addresses in general terms but does not operationalise. LAIF also requires self-application: the governance body applying LAIF is itself subject to the same standards.
MAP(Risk Identification and Context) | Identify the organisational context, categories of risk, and the AI system's risk profile. Understand how the system will be used and by whom. Identify affected groups and potential harms. | PDCA Section B (Coherence Test Documentation) — requires identification of the specific human interest at stake, identification of all substantially irreversible consequences, and completion of coupling and consistency analysis. PDCA Section C — identifies the affected persons and the specific provisions engaged. | LAIF's coupling documentation (B.1) requires that for every risk identified, the corresponding protection of equivalent normative force is also identified. NIST AI RMF's Map function identifies risks; LAIF requires the Map function to also confirm that protections are structurally adequate. This is the most significant structural addition.
MEASURE(Risk Analysis and Assessment) | Analyse and assess identified risks. Measure system performance. Assess trustworthiness. Document findings with appropriate metrics. | PDCA Section A (Integrity Layer Verification) — requires calibration testing, adversarial testing, boundary testing, and documentation of results against the three Integrity Layer properties. Toolkit Module 1 provides operational measurement standards for each property. | LAIF's adversarial consistency requirement — that the system performs consistently whether or not it is being evaluated — is more specific than NIST AI RMF's trustworthiness measurement guidance. LAIF also requires that the explanation produced by the system reliably reflects the actual basis of the output, not merely that an explanation can be produced.
MANAGE(Risk Response and Monitoring) | Prioritise and address identified risks. Implement risk response plans. Monitor for new risks and changes in risk. Document risk decisions. | PDCA Toolkit Module 3 (Ongoing Monitoring Protocol) — provides specific monitoring requirements: discrimination monitoring (quarterly minimum), containment integrity review (monthly minimum), reversibility incident tracking, and annual PDCA review. Toolkit Module 4 (Enforcement Reference Model) provides the three-level violation classification and restoration requirements. | LAIF's monitoring protocol is more operationally specific than NIST AI RMF's Manage function guidance. The minimum frequency requirements (quarterly, monthly), the specific monitoring categories (discrimination, containment, reversibility incidents), and the three-level escalation protocol are more prescriptive than anything in NIST AI RMF 1.0. Organisations that have adopted the NIST AI RMF can treat the PDCA as the Manage function's primary documentation instrument.

For federal agencies procuring AI systems under OMB M-24-18, the following describes how to integrate LAIF PDCA completion as a vendor qualification and evaluation standard. This integration requires no new legislation: it is implemented through existing procurement instruments.

Immediate Action for US Federal Agencies  The OMB M-24-10 impact assessment requirement for AI systems affecting rights or safety is the immediate integration point. Agencies that are conducting these assessments can adopt the LAIF PDCA-Full as the document format for the impact assessment — it satisfies all OMB M-24-10 requirements and adds the coupling, consistency, and reversibility analysis that OMB does not specify. This requires no new authority: it is a format choice within existing impact assessment obligations. Agencies that adopt this approach immediately generate the PDCA documentation base needed for LAIF certification and safe harbour protection.
PART THREE — PRACTICAL IMPLEMENTATION QUESTIONS
Q: We are already compliant with the EU AI Act. How much additional work does LAIF require?  A: The additional work depends on how thoroughly your existing compliance process has completed the substantive analysis rather than just the documentation. If your Article 9 risk management process has rigorously identified what specific human interests each risk measure is designed to protect, LAIF Section B.1 will take approximately two to four hours to document for a straightforward deployment. If — as is common — your Article 9 process has identified risks without explicitly identifying the coupled protections, Section B.1 may take one to two days and may surface structural gaps that need remediation. In our experience applying the coupling question to existing EU AI Act documentation, the most common finding is that risk mitigation measures exist but the specific human interest each measure protects has not been explicitly documented. This is a documentation gap, not necessarily a substantive governance gap — but the documentation process often reveals that the protection is weaker than the restriction it is supposed to counterbalance.
Q: We use the NIST AI RMF. Does LAIF replace it?  A: No. LAIF and the NIST AI RMF are designed to work together. The NIST AI RMF provides the organisational governance structure and risk management process; LAIF provides the structural standard — the three questions the risk management process must be able to answer. An organisation that has adopted the NIST AI RMF can treat the PDCA as the primary documentation instrument for the Govern, Map, Measure, and Manage functions. The PDCA's Section A satisfies the Measure function; Section B satisfies the Map function; Section C satisfies the Manage function; Section D satisfies the Govern function. You do not need to maintain two separate documentation systems: the PDCA is the NIST AI RMF documentation in LAIF format.
Q: We are a small developer. PDCA-Full sounds extremely heavy. What do we actually have to do?  A: If your system's outputs do not materially affect individual interests in consequential domains — meaning they do not affect people's liberty, health, employment, education, or welfare benefits — you qualify for PDCA-Lite. PDCA-Lite requires only Section A (Integrity Layer Verification, which is a checklist with evidence requirements) and Section B.1 (coupling documentation, which is one to two pages identifying what human interest your deployment serves and what protects it). That is a morning's work for a technically competent team. PDCA-Full is required only where the stakes justify it. For a system that materially affects the liberty or health of individuals, the documentation burden of PDCA-Full is proportionate to the stakes involved — and it is substantially less burdensome than the regulatory consequences of a Level 3 structural violation.
Q: How does LAIF interact with the safe harbour? If I complete the PDCA and something still goes wrong, am I protected?  A: The safe harbour protects operators who completed the PDCA in good faith and maintained the monitoring protocol — it does not protect against all liability. Specifically: if your PDCA identified a structural risk and you deployed anyway without adequate compensating protections, the safe harbour does not apply. If your PDCA was thorough and accurate, you deployed based on its findings, and an unforeseen harm occurred that the PDCA process would not have identified even if conducted competently and in good faith, the safe harbour applies. The test is structural due diligence, not zero risk. Governance frameworks do not guarantee harm-free outcomes; they establish the conditions under which an operator has exercised appropriate judgment. The safe harbour codifies what adequate judgment looks like under LAIF v1.2.
Q: We want to adopt LAIF but our regulator has not. Can we still get benefit from it?  A: Yes, through two mechanisms. First, corporate governance adoption: commit at board level to completing PDCA-Full for all high-stakes deployments, include PDCA outcomes in your annual AI governance reporting, and subject your PDCA process to periodic independent review as part of your external audit programme. This creates the documentation base that satisfies the safe harbour conditions under LAIF v1.2 even before your regulator has formally adopted it, and positions you well for procurement conditioning when your public sector clients begin requiring PDCA completion. Second, procurement conditioning: if any of your clients are public sector bodies, begin offering PDCA completion as a vendor qualification demonstration. Public sector procurement officers who understand the OMB M-24-18 impact assessment requirements will recognise the PDCA as satisfying those requirements and will regard PDCA-complete vendors as lower-risk procurement choices.
Q: What is the relationship between LAIF certification and existing product certifications (CE marking, FDA clearance)?  A: LAIF certification operates in parallel with, and at a different level from, product-specific certifications. CE marking and FDA clearance assess whether a specific AI product meets the technical and safety standards for its intended medical or consumer purpose. LAIF certification assesses whether the operator's deployment process satisfies the structural governance conditions — coupling, consistency, reversibility — for the specific deployment configuration. A product can have CE marking and fail LAIF certification (if the deployment configuration violates Provision C3, for example). A product can have LAIF certification for one deployment context and not another (certification is at the deployment level, not the product level). The two certifications address different questions and both may be required.
